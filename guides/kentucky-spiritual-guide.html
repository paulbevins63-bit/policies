Kentucky Spiritual Guide

Finding your way through faith, recovery, and the help around you.

This guide walks through every button and every screen — what it does, where your information goes, and how to get the most from the app. Nothing here is complicated; take it a section at a time. If you only read one thing, read the disclaimers at the end — they matter.

Companion note: wherever this guide mentions Abe or “your companion,” that’s the optional AI helper delivered by the separate Kentucky MASH AI app. Spirit works fully without it — those parts simply stay hidden until MASH is installed.

Part 2

Prayers — and how to find them

Tap Prayers on the home screen to open Types of Prayers, then Guidance and Support to reach the prayer list, titled My Prayers.

The app ships with 21 built-in prayers spanning many traditions — labeled in each prayer as Protestant, Catholic, Greek / Russian / Ethiopian Orthodox, or “All Christians.” They include the Prayer of Deliverance, St. Patrick’s Breastplate, the Serenity Prayer (short and complete forms), the Jesus Prayer, the Suscipe, a Daily Thanksgiving prayer, and more. Each prayer has two tabs at the top — Modern 2026 (plain, present-day wording) and Original Historic (the traditional text).

New prayers arrive over time

The prayer collection grows. New prayers are added periodically with app updates — so it’s worth checking back. You can also add your own: the Add / Import button lets you import a prayer from a .txt file or from Google Drive, and your custom prayers can be deleted anytime.

“How do I search for a prayer?”

Honest answer: prayers aren’t searched — they’re browsed. There’s no keyword search inside the Prayers area; you find a prayer by scanning the grid of titles on the My Prayers screen. (Keyword search does exist, but it’s a Bible feature — see Part 4.) If you’re looking for a specific prayer, open My Prayers and read down the list; the titles tell you what each one is.

Part 3

The Bible & installing offline Bibles

Tap Bibles to open the Bible screen. At the top is a search box (Search keywords… with a gold GO), plus Bookmarks and History buttons. Two kinds of Bibles live here:

Online Bibles (internet required)

A panel of well-known translations — NIV, ESV, NLT, RSV, NABRE, NJB, NAB and more — that open on BibleGateway.com. These need a connection; the app links out to them so the publishers’ copyrights stay with the publishers. Tapping Other Bibles reveals five more online versions: NKJV, NASB, NRSV, CSB, and the Jerusalem Bible (JB).

Offline Bibles (no internet, once installed)

Four complete Bibles are already installed and work with no connection at all: King James Version, Berean Literal Bible, Catholic Public Domain Version, and Douay-Rheims (Complete).

Many more can be added for free. Tap Other Bibles, and you’ll find three sections:

  • More Offline Bibles — 10 complete Bibles (full Old and New Testament): ASV with Strong’s, Berean Standard, Bishops’, Coverdale, Darby, Bible in Basic English, World English Bible, Geneva, KJV with Strong’s, and The NET Bible.
  • Partial Offline Bibles — Old-Testament-only (Brenton Septuagint, JPS Tanakh 1917), New-Testament-only (Revised Young’s Literal NT), and incomplete works (Open English Bible, Tyndale Bible). Each one explains exactly which books are included right under its button.
  • More Online Bibles — the five extra BibleGateway versions noted above.
How to install an offline Bible

Tap any Bible that isn’t installed yet. A window titled Not Installed appears with a short note about what’s included and asks: “Install {name} now? It’s free and works fully offline once installed.” Tap Yes, Install. A brief spinner shows while it loads onto your phone — no internet is used, the text is already bundled in the app. When it’s done, the Bible gets a ✓ and moves to the top of the list; tap it to start reading. To undo, tap Not Now instead.

An Aa button opens a Text Size slider (12–32 pt) so you can make the words as large as you need.

Jumping to a chapter

While you read, the book title at the top is a button — it says “tap for chapters” right beneath it. Tap it and a grid of every chapter number appears, already scrolled to where you are; tap a number to jump straight there. Psalm 140 is one tap away, not a hundred swipes. The ‹ and › arrows beside the title step one chapter at a time.

Part 4

Searching Bible passages

The search box lives at the top of the Bibles screen. Type a word or phrase into Search keywords… and tap GO.

  • The Search Results screen shows each match as “Book chapter:verse” in gold with the verse text beneath. Tap any result to jump straight to that verse in the reader.
  • Search runs against the offline King James Version index on your phone — so it works with no internet. Any offline Bible you’ve installed can be read fully offline as well.
  • If you (or your companion) mention a reference like “James 1:5,” the app can pull that verse straight from the offline KJV without a search.

Remember: this keyword search is for the Bible. Prayers are browsed by title (Part 2).

Part 5

Recovery Readings & Church Readings

Recovery Readings

These are daily recovery meditations. You can reach them two ways:

  • The Recovery Readings button on the home screen (left arm of the cross), and
  • Inside Recovery, via the Daily Readings button.

Both open the same list. It gathers well-known daily readings — AA Daily Reflections, NA Just For Today and Spiritual Principle a Day, several Al-Anon readings, Hazelden meditations (Twenty-Four Hours a Day, Touchstones, Today’s Gift, and more), plus a small “Other Daily Readings” section (Daily Zen, Buddhist Daily Wisdom). Each opens in the app’s reader, and — when your companion is installed — with the in-reading companion bar (Part 12).

Church Readings

Tap Church Readings (right arm of the cross) for daily lectionary and devotional readings, grouped under headers by tradition: Anglican, Baptist, Catholic, Christian in General, Episcopalian, Lutheran, Presbyterian, and Orthodox. The Catholic section is the largest — Mass readings from the USCCB, homilies, Sacred Space, several devotionals, and a Franciscan Media set. Two Catholic items — Order of the Mass and Order USCCB — open as offline, in-app readers (the full order of Mass, section by section), so you can follow along with no connection.

On the home screen and inside Recovery

Recovery Readings appear in both places on purpose — from the home cross and again inside the Recovery menu — so a daily reading is never more than a tap or two away.

Part 6

Churches & the four location buttons

The Churches screen finds places of worship near you. Along the top is a location dock with four buttons. Here’s exactly what each does.

ButtonWhat happens when you tap it
My Location Uses GPS to find where you are. The first time, it shows a short notice (“Use your location?”) explaining that your location is sent to OpenStreetMap only to search for nearby places — tap Allow to continue, or Not now to back out. You’ll see status like “Connecting to GPS…” then “Location: {city}, {state}.” (More on this in Part 10.)
Zip Code Opens Enter 5-Digit Zip Code. Type a ZIP (e.g. 42701) and tap Search — no GPS needed. Works even offline, using the ZIP database built into the app.
Select State Opens a Select Region list (United States / Canada) so you can browse an area by hand, with no location access at all.
Global Search Opens the Resource Search screen — free 211 help and a national services directory (covered in Part 7).

Results start within about 20 miles and are sorted nearest-first. A Expand Search (50 mi) button widens the radius. Church data comes from two places: IRS-registered non-profit records bundled by state, and the OpenStreetMap community map. A Faith: filter lets you narrow to a tradition (All Faiths, Orthodox, Baptist, Catholic, Methodist, and so on). Cards show the church name, a faith label, a green Offers Assistance badge when a congregation helps the needy, a tappable address that opens maps, and website links. You can tap Not there to flag a listing that’s wrong.

Call before you go

The app itself reminds you: “Call before you go — service times can change.” Some listings come from community maps and may be outdated; rows that look questionable are marked “verify before going.” Always confirm before making a trip.

“Churches” is not “Church Aid”

The home-screen Churches button lists all churches near you. The Church Aid button (inside Personal Assistance, Part 7) is different — it lists only churches that offer help like food, clothing, shelter, or jobs. Same neighborhood, different purpose.

Adding a resource yourself

Add Resources lets you save a free or low-cost resource so others can find it — it stays on your phone. Four ways in:

  • Scan a Page — photograph a flyer or listing; the app reads the text on-device and pulls out the details.
  • Upload a File — read a file already on your phone.
  • Browse a Website — paste a web address and the app pulls out the name, phone, and address.
  • Manually Enter or Edit Data — type it in or fix it yourself, choosing a category.

Global Search (the 211 screen)

Global Search opens Resource Search. At the top is 211 — free help: Call 211 (Free) (dials the free 24/7 helpline), Text 211 (Free), and Kentucky 211 Online. Type a term and you also get Search FindHelp (Free) — a national directory of free/reduced-cost services. Below that, On-Device Results searches the resources saved on your phone. An optional Enhanced Search exists for people who can pay a provider’s API fees directly — it’s clearly marked optional and off by default.

Verify before you go — especially crisis resources

The app is blunt about this: “Verify all resources before you go. Listings may be outdated.” For crisis categories it goes further — “⚠ CALL TO VERIFY BEFORE YOU GO. Crisis resources change fast. Make sure your shelter is safe, open, and right for you. DO NOT GO without confirming.” Please take that seriously.

Part 9

Your Notebook & personal information

Tap Notebook to open My Journal. The first time, a short “Welcome to Your Journal” form asks for the basics — a preferred name, your location, and a recovery phone / main contact. After that you get the journal composer: an optional title, a big “What’s on your mind today?” box, and a Submit Entry button. Recent entries appear as chips you can reopen.

What the Notebook is for

Two things: a place to write and keep private journal entries, and a personal profile that helps your companion (if you use one) speak to you like it actually knows you. Tap Personal Info (top-right) to open the full profile.

Why the profile asks what it asks

Right at the top, the app makes a promise, word for word:

The app’s own promise

“Everything here is optional and stays on your phone. The more you share, the better your companion can walk alongside you — in your own language, at your own pace.”

Every field is optional. The profile includes: Basics (what to call you, location, phone); Milestones (clean date, birthday — dates only, so your companion can acknowledge the day without you raising it); Memories (facts your companion has learned, which you can view, edit, or delete); About Me (family, pets, hobbies, favorite books / music / movies, meaningful places); Faith (your background, in your own words); and How to Talk With Me (whether you prefer plain talk or faith language, stories or step-by-step, gentle or straight — and lines you never want crossed). None of it is required; the more you fill in, the more personal the companion feels. That’s the entire reason it’s collected — to serve you, on your device.

There’s also an Emergency Info / Medical ID editor (reachable from the home strip). It holds emergency contacts and optional medical details (blood type, allergies, medications, conditions, physician). It promises: “This stays on your phone. If something happens to you, these contacts and details show on the app’s front page so help can find them.” A Share button builds a plain-text summary and opens Android’s share sheet — sharing only ever happens when you tap it.

Part 10

“Exact Location” — how it’s used, and why

When you tap My Location, the app uses your phone’s GPS to get a precise position. Here’s the whole story, plainly.

  • What it’s used for: (1) turning your position into a city and state to show you; (2) sorting and filtering nearby churches and resources by distance on your phone; and (3) asking OpenStreetMap’s public map for places near you.
  • What actually leaves your phone: only the latitude and longitude, inside the map search. No name, no account, nothing that identifies you rides along with it.
  • You’re always asked first. The location notice appears after you tap My Location and before Android’s permission prompt. Its exact words:
The location notice, word for word

“To find churches and services near you, this app sends your location to OpenStreetMap’s Overpass servers to search for places nearby. Your location is used only for that search — never stored off your phone or tied to you.”

If you’d rather not use GPS at all, use Zip Code or Select State instead — both find places without any location access.

Part 11

Where your information lives

The short version: your information stays on your phone. The app is built so that journals, profile, recovery contacts, and emergency info are stored locally and are deliberately excluded from cloud backup — they don’t get swept into Google’s automatic backup. Nothing is uploaded to a server the app runs, because there isn’t one.

Spirit’s data vs. the companion’s data

There are two separate places information can live, and it helps to know the difference:

Kentucky Spiritual GuideKentucky MASH AI (the companion)
HoldsYour journal, personal profile, prayers, bookmarks, recovery contacts, saved resources, emergency info.Your conversations with the companion, and the facts it remembers, kept per companion.
WhereLocal databases on your phone.An encrypted local database on your phone.
Leaves the device?No (only map coordinates for a nearby search — Part 10).No — conversations never leave the device.
How the two share memory

When you use the companion, Spirit can share the facts you chose to save (your name, the profile you filled in) so the companion speaks to you personally. Just as importantly, anything the companion learns about you can be carried back and used by the companion inside the Spiritual Guide — so when you ask Abe about a reading in Spirit, it already knows what it learned in a MASH conversation. Spirit keeps the master copy of these “memories,” and you can view, edit, or delete every one of them on the Memories screen. Sharing is governed by a toggle in your profile (Introduce me when I open Abe); turn it off and nothing personal rides along.

What the companion can and can’t do — inside Spirit

Inside the Spiritual Guide, the companion works quietly in the background. When it’s installed, readings show an in-reading bar with Relate this to me and Relate this to the Bible, and the journal shows an Interact strip. It can answer about the passage you’re reading, remember your name and preferences, and carry its memories with it. What it can’t do: it can’t reach the internet for answers, it has no account and does nothing in the cloud, and if the MASH app isn’t installed these companion features simply stay hidden — Spirit keeps working exactly as before. (If you don’t have MASH, the “Ask” popup still answers from the app’s own built-in library of passages.)
